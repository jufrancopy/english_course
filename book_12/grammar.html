Conditionals (If-Clauses)
Lesson 1: Expressing future probability.

First Conditional

We use the First Conditional to talk about a realistic situation in the present or future and its likely result.

Structure: If + Present Simple, ... will + base verb.

Examples:

If-Clause (Condition)Main Clause (Result)
If it rains today,we won't go to the park.
If he works tonight,John won't be able to meet us.
If I have time,I'll go to the store.

Present Perfect
Lesson 1 & 2: For experiences and the indefinite past.

Present Perfect Tense

We use the Present Perfect (have/has + past participle) to talk about past actions when the time is not specified (indefinite past) or for life experiences.

Examples:

Indefinite Past: Ann has made up her mind. (We don't know exactly when).

Experience: Have you ever gone to South America?

With "since": Bill has known Mary since they were children.

Adverbs
Lesson 2: still, yet, already, no longer...

Common Adverbs of Time

These adverbs help specify the time of an action, especially with the present perfect.

  • Still: An action or situation continues to the present. (e.g., "She still works there.")
  • Yet: In questions and negatives, about something that hasn't happened. (e.g., "He hasn't seen the doctor yet.")
  • Already: Something happened sooner than expected. (e.g., "He has already left.")
  • No longer: Something that was true in the past is not true now. (e.g., "He no longer lives here.")

Conjunctions
Lesson 2: Connecting ideas with 'so' and 'or'.

Coordinate Conjunctions: SO and OR

We use conjunctions to connect words, phrases, and clauses.

  • SO: Shows a result or consequence. (Cause -> so -> Result)
  • OR: Shows a choice or alternative. (Option 1 or Option 2)

Examples:

SO: John was sick, so he went to a doctor.

OR: Ron can drive to work, or he could ride the bus.

Modals (Could)
Lesson 3: Expressing possibility.

Using 'Could' for Possibility

We use could to suggest a possibility in the present or future. It's similar to 'might' or 'may'.

Structure: Subject + could + base verb.

Examples:

Where is Tom? -> He could be at work.

She could have a doctor's appointment this afternoon.

Adjectives (-ing vs. -ed)
Lesson 3: Describing things and feelings.

-ing vs. -ed Adjectives

  • Adjectives ending in -ing describe the thing, person, or situation that CAUSES a feeling. (e.g., a boring movie)
  • Adjectives ending in -ed describe the FEELING itself. (e.g., a bored person)

Examples:

The commercials are not interesting. I am not interested.

Tom's new job is exciting. Tom is excited about his new job.

Reported Speech
Lesson 3: Reporting what someone said.

Indirect Speech with 'said'

When we report what someone said, we often move the verb tense one step into the past. This is called "backshift".

Structure: Subject + said + (that) + subject + past tense verb.

Examples:

Direct SpeechReported (Indirect) Speech
She says, "I am going."She said (that) she was going.
They say, "We are going."They said (that) they were going.

Past Habits (used to / would)
Lesson 4: Talking about past routines.

'Used to' and 'Would'

  • Used to: Describes a past habit or state that is now finished. It works for actions and states.
  • Would: Describes a repeated past action. It is often used for nostalgic memories and does not work for states (like 'live' or 'be').

Examples:

I used to spend hours doing homework. (Now I don't).

When I was a child, I would go to the beach every summer.

I used to live in the city. (Cannot use 'would' here, because 'live' is a state).

Comparative Adverbs
Lesson 4: Comparing actions.

Comparing Adverbs with -er

To compare how two people do an action, we can add -er to short adverbs. This is often followed by than.

Examples:

Pam drives faster than Tim does.

He runs slower than his brother.
